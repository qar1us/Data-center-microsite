02 For Residents

Three ways to get involved.

01

Get the facts

Information is a powerful tool. Most state laws let you request records, including non-disclosure agreements (NDAs) and contracts. Ask a local civil society organization for a cost-benefit analysis of what the project means for your rates.

02

Participate in the discussion

Join a local hearing or take part in a petition. Ask for a breakdown of contract terms and tax exemptions before any vote.

03

Know your options

Formal channels are open to you: comment in Public Utility Commission proceedings, weigh in on local ballot measures, and vote for the commissioners who set your utility rates.

03 In Practice

How communities are engaging.

Across the country, residents and local officials are using these tools to different ends. Here’s where residents have secured local benefits, negotiated transparency, protected ratepayers, and put decisions to a public vote.

West Des Moines, IA

Officials negotiated an agreement with Microsoft that secured 100% renewable operation and more than $2 billion in tax revenue for the community.

Port Washington, WI

Residents gathered roughly 1,000 signatures and passed a ballot measure giving voters a say before the city grants future data center tax incentives.

Pima County, AZ

After Project Blue, the county adopted a 90-day public-review period, capped economic-development NDA terms at two years, and now discloses active NDAs quarterly.

Minnesota

A community group intervened before the Public Utility Commission, which directed an Amazon proposal through a full regulatory review.

04 Why It Matters

What’s actually at stake.

Your bills

Utilities often socialize the cost of grid and transmission upgrades across all ratepayers. Abandoned projects can leave you stranded with the costs.
